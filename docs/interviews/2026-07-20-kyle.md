### Kyle's Coffee Profile (Pre-App Interview)

- **Home setup:** Chemex pour over; currently jerry-rigging with a filter in a baking sieve over a Pyrex (filters for the new two-cup Chemex haven't arrived). **Fellow kettle** singled out as the single best home-brew investment (precise flow control)
- **Out vs. home:** prefers going *out* for coffee (cold brew at Cup of Joy) over making it at home; home brewing is "utilitarian" — "I just need caffeine in my veins"
- **Currently at home:** Trader Joe's coffee — acknowledges specialty makes "a huge difference" but doesn't chase it at home
- **History:** buying specialty on and off ~6–8 years; ~$25–50/month, usually 1–2 bags
- **Most memorable recent buy:** Cat and Cloud, Santa Cruz
- **Quality split:** ~5–10% "incredible"; the rest fine-to-disappointing, sometimes due to brewing execution rather than the bean

### Key Insights (Pre-App)

- **Variety is a barrier to home brewing.** Kyle gets bored drinking the same coffee for two weeks; would prefer smaller quantities of multiple coffees, or a mix-and-match pack ("buy three different bags at once, use them all, buy three more")
- **Pour-over flavor reward is marginal vs. milk-based drinks.** Appreciating the notes "has to be appreciated with a different part of your brain… that part gets over the novelty really fast." Contrast: a frou-frou latte is "objectively good" like cake; a pour over's cherry note is a subtle, effortful pleasure. "I've never craved an Ethiopian to get through a stressful day."
   - **Open question Kyle raised himself:** could deliberate palate development change that? "If I really focused on my palate… would it actually get there?" — the exact thesis Palato is built on
- **Biggest frustration:** coffee nomenclature is vague/unreliable — "It says mango, there's no guarantee you're actually going to taste that"

### App UX Walkthrough

**Onboarding**

- Engaging, but **none of the "what intrigues you" options matched his motivation** — he was looking for *"I want to get better at knowing what I like."*
- The sign-in/save (OAuth) link "blue screen" was noticed but didn't alarm him ("Is that link really scary to you?" → "No")

**Logging a coffee (add-and-rate)**

- Used a photo from his library to scan a bag; OCR filled some fields but not all (sparse bag text)
- Noted the flow requires info (e.g. process / wash method) that isn't always on the bag
- **Duplicate detection worked correctly** and surfaced a clear message
- **Bug:** hitting the back button after the duplicate warning sent him back to the **login screen** instead of the catalog (and the bottom nav briefly disappeared)
- Overall: "felt thorough, trustworthy, and intriguing — I have a sense of hope and trust in this app" — though slightly long (acknowledged the length is partly the price of good data)
- **SWE instinct:** wondered aloud how the app validates data to avoid incorrect duplicates

**Palate + Recommendations**

- **Key request:** wanted to see a **fully built-out example palate** *before* logging enough to unlock his own — "to understand what I'm working toward." Suggested sample/demo data ("here's what you're growing into")
- **Recommendation labels:** "Try Something Unique" felt disconnected from his data ("is this connected to me? I'm not sure"); leading with **"Something You'll Love"** would signal personalization
- Wanted an interpretive/"personality-test" layer to the palate reveal

### Social Features Feedback

- Clear interest in **lightweight** social — not full social media, but the ability to **follow specific people and view their palates**
- Suggested a notification-style hook: *"Jesse just drank a coffee that's super similar to one you rated highly — you should try it"*
- Framed as curiosity about how people in his life taste differently, given how personalized/data-rich each profile becomes
- *Jesse noted this validates a social layer he'd been on the fence about*

### Monetization Discussion

- **Affiliate model:** Jesse described earning a percentage when users click through to buy directly from a roaster via Palato-tracked links (UTM). Kyle understood it as a marketing engine for roasters
- **Subscription ($3.99–5.99/month):** Jesse leaning against it; Kyle agreed he **wouldn't pay** but would keep using it if free and engaging — compared it to Duolingo ("I'd use it every day but I won't pay for it")
- **Kyle's idea — personalized coffee subscription:** ~$20/month, a bag each month matched to your tasting profile, with a **pre-shipment notification** asking whether to match your profile or try something new. He'd pay for a well-executed version *because existing subscriptions (Blue Bottle, etc.) lack the personalization*
   - Jesse surfaced **Trade Coffee**, which already does a version of this via an initial quiz; Jesse's read is Trade's recs can feel generic (large roasters like Stumptown). Kyle's counter: "hey, that's good for you if you ever choose to compete"
- **"Palato Certified" B2B model:** Jesse exploring partnerships with small, quality-focused micro-roasters (e.g. Frank), promoting their coffees for a fee / rev share. Named tension: big brands (Blue Bottle) would pay more but don't align with the mission of surfacing small roasters. Kyle affirmed he'd pay for a well-executed personalized subscription

## Small / anecdotal observations (worth keeping)

- **The motivation the quiz can't hear (n=2 with Jeremy).** Kyle's actual reason — "I want to get better at knowing what I like" — isn't a quiz option. Jeremy's missing option was "keep track of my coffee memories." Two different Round 2 users, two different core motivations, *both invisible to the motivation question.* The quiz's fixed motivation set is now a replicated gap.
- **The whole thesis, spoken by a skeptic.** Kyle is the sample's clearest "the flavor reward is marginal" voice — and *he's the one who asked whether focused palate development could change that* ("if I really focused on my palate… would it actually get there?"). Jesse: "That's what I'm building." This is the single most useful validation-or-refutation target in the round: Palato's premise is that the answer is yes. Kyle is a natural longitudinal case.
- **"Something You'll Love" should lead.** Watching the three rec cards, Kyle read "Try Something Unique" first and couldn't tell if it was personalized ("this could be anywhere… is this connected to me?"). Leading with "Something You'll Love" would immediately signal *this is from your data*. Concrete, cheap ordering fix with a clear rationale.
- **He wants the destination before the journey.** The strongest unprompted product ask: show a fleshed-out example palate *before* the 3-rating unlock — "I'm craving to see what someone else's palate looks like… bait it a little, here's what you're growing into." Doubles as his on-ramp to the social layer (seeing *someone else's* profile) and as an activation fix for the empty-state problem Jono hit from the other side.
- **Social = follow specific people, not a feed.** Explicitly *not* Instagram/Venmo social. He wants to search a profile ("what's Jesse been drinking?"), and a notification when someone he follows drinks something close to a coffee he rated highly. The "why" is grounded: *because the profile is so data-rich*, other people's profiles become genuinely interesting. This is the second Round 2 request adjacent to a social layer (Jeremy asked for influencer/Letterboxd-style profiles) — the theme is converging.
- **Variety-as-barrier is a subscription spec.** His boredom with two weeks of one coffee → wanting a mix-and-match / multi-bag pack → his $20/month profile-matched subscription idea with a pre-ship "match me or surprise me" prompt. Notably, his invented subscription is *nearly identical to the app's own three-rec framing* ("Something You'll Love / Try Something Unique / Go Somewhere New") — he reinvented the product's recommendation logic as a fulfillment model, unprompted.
- **The Trade Coffee benchmark.** Jesse walked Kyle through Trade's onboarding quiz live. Kyle's takeaway: Trade already does the quiz-to-subscription flow, but "less detailed," and Jesse's experience is the quality is "lackluster mega-roaster" (Stumptown-tier). This is the clearest competitor to study for the subscription path — action-item'd. The differentiation wedge Kyle names: personalization depth + small-roaster curation.
- **"Consumer-led B2B."** Jesse's own framing of the model, captured here for the record: free to users always (don't sell user data), monetize via affiliate purchases and/or "Palato Certified" roaster partnerships — surfacing small roasters (Frank) over big ones (Blue Bottle, "will never pass through the gates here"), with the explicit acknowledgment that it's a hard business case *because* Blue Bottle would always pay more than Frank could.
- **The duplicate-detection win.** Amid the bugs, worth logging that duplicate detection *worked* and showed a clear "already in your catalog" message — the add-flow's dedupe is doing its job; the defect is only the back-nav after it.
- **Data-integrity instinct (pro user tell):** "My brain is going slightly into software-engineer mode — is there some way to validate the data? You could end up with duplicates that are incorrect from one another." A coffee professional worrying about *catalog quality* — the exact concern the moderation/verification gate exists to answer.
- **Trust language, unprompted:** "I have a sense of hope and trust in this app currently that hasn't let me down yet." A strong, quotable trust signal from a professional — even while calling the flow "a little thorough."
