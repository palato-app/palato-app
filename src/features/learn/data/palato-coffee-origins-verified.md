# Palato: Coffee Origins (Verified, Arabica-Focused)

**Status:** v1.2: verified build, global coverage complete (elevation bands, sourcing standard, Réunion and Mauritius folded into Africa) **Method:** Each country is verified before inclusion. Varietals are reported at the country level, since region-level varietal splits are mostly unpublished and unreliable. Region-level notes appear only where genuinely distinct and verifiable.

**Sourcing.** Priority goes to national coffee authorities (Anacafé, ICAFE, IHCAFE, Cenicafé, and the like), World Coffee Research, the SCA, and peer-reviewed or governmental data, then to established specialty importers with origin desks. General-reference sites are treated as a lead only, and any load-bearing fact is checked against a primary source before it is published. No fact is invented; anything uncertain is flagged.

**Altitude.** The altitude line is the verified coffee-growing band, meaning the elevation where coffee is actually grown, not the terrain range of the wider administrative region. A region inherits its country band unless a specific verified figure is given in its own line. No per-region elevation is synthesized from maps. (Open digital elevation models can describe the land, but not where on that land coffee grows; that is a separate, clearly-labeled enrichment, never a coffee-altitude source.)

### Inclusion & formatting rules

- Arabica-only country: country-level varietals, an altitude band, and a list of Arabica-producing regions.  
- Both Arabica and Robusta: keep only the Arabica regions; any Robusta-dominant zone collapses to a single "Produces Robusta" line.  
- Robusta-only country: name plus "Produces Robusta," no regions. (Style differently on the map.)  
- Inclusion gate: if trustworthy country-level varietal data cannot be found, the country is cut rather than guessed. Cut countries still get an emerging or historical blurb if they have a story worth teaching.  
- No invented data. Anything uncertain is flagged.  
- The **Emerging** label flags a region or country with a notably young, reviving, or fast-changing specialty scene (a learning hook for users).

## SOUTH AMERICA

### Colombia *(Arabica, \~99% of production)*

**Common varietals:** Castillo (now most widely planted), Caturra, Colombia (Variedad Colombia), Typica, Bourbon, Tabi, Maragogipe. Specialty pockets: Geisha, Pink Bourbon, Pacamara. **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-2,000 m (some departments climb toward 2,300 m).

**Arabica regions:**

- Huila  
- Nariño  
- Cauca  
- Tolima  
- Antioquia  
- Caldas  
- Quindío  
- Risaralda  
- Santander  
- Norte de Santander  
- Cundinamarca  
- Boyacá  
- Valle del Cauca  
- Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta (Magdalena, Cesar, La Guajira)

*(Caldas, Quindío, and Risaralda make up the Eje Cafetero, the Coffee Triangle.)*

---

### Brazil *(both; world's largest producer, Arabica plus Conilon Robusta)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Mundo Novo, Catuaí (Red and Yellow), Bourbon (incl. Yellow Bourbon), Acaiá, Icatu, Obatã, Topázio, Catucaí. Maragogipe in pockets. **Altitude:** roughly 800-1,400 m. Much of the crop sits between 900 and 1,200 m, while the Mantiqueira and Caparaó specialty zones reach about 1,400 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Minas Gerais:** Sul de Minas, Cerrado Mineiro, Chapada de Minas, Matas de Minas, Caparaó  
- **São Paulo:** Mogiana / Alta Mogiana  
- **Bahia:** Chapada Diamantina, Cerrado/Planalto  
- **Espírito Santo:** Montanhas do Espírito Santo only (the mountainous Arabica zone)  
- **Paraná**

**Emerging.** Caparaó (on the Minas and Espírito Santo border) and high-altitude Bahia are the newer specialty frontiers within an otherwise mature origin.

**Produces Robusta (Conilon):** lowland Espírito Santo (\~80% of state output), Rondônia, Acre, southern Bahia.

### Peru *(Arabica, nearly all production)*

**Common varietals:** Typica (locally "Nacional," historically the majority), Caturra, Bourbon, Pache, Catimor. Specialty: Catuaí, Geisha. **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-2,000 m (Cajamarca 1,600-2,000 m; Cusco 1,500-1,800 m; Puno climbing to 2,000 m).

**Arabica regions:**

- Cajamarca (Jaén, San Ignacio, Cutervo)  
- Amazonas  
- San Martín  
- Junín (Chanchamayo, Satipo)  
- Cusco (La Convención)  
- Puno (Sandia)  
- Pasco (Villa Rica)  
- Huánuco  
- Ayacucho  
- Piura (Huancabamba)

**Emerging.** Cusco and Ayacucho are up-and-coming high-altitude zones where producers are replacing rust-driven Catimor with quality varieties (Typica, Bourbon, Geisha) and beginning to place in Cup of Excellence.

---

### Bolivia *(Arabica, all production)*

**Common varietals:** Typica, Caturra, Catuaí, Criollo, Pacas. Emerging specialty: Geisha, Java. **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-1,700 m across the Yungas. Finca Takesi, the highest known coffee farm in the world, climbs to about 2,600 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- Caranavi *(the Yungas carry \~90% of national production, with Caranavi as the hub)*  
- Nor Yungas and Sud Yungas  
- Coroico  
- Larecaja  
- Franz Tamayo  
- Santa Cruz (Samaipata, Valle Grande, Ichilo)  
- Cochabamba (Chapare)  
- Tarija  
- Beni  
- Pando

**Emerging.** South America's only landlocked, smallest-volume origin. A quality-driven revival (the Sol de la Mañana program, exotic Geisha and Java plantings) is rebuilding output after a long productivity slump.

---

### Ecuador *(both; roughly 50/50 Arabica and Robusta by volume)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Typica, Caturra, Bourbon. Notable specialty: Sidra, Geisha. Colombia and Castillo imported in the north; some SL28, Sarchimor. **Altitude:** highland zones roughly 1,200-2,000 m. The Galápagos are a rare near-sea-level exception, growing coffee around 300 m thanks to the cool Humboldt current.

**Arabica regions:**

- Loja *(highest-grade Arabica; Vilcabamba, Malacatos, Catamayo)*  
- Zamora-Chinchipe (Nangaritza, Palanda)  
- El Oro (Zaruma)  
- Pichincha (Pacto)  
- Imbabura (Intag Valley)  
- Carchi  
- Manabí *(coastal; mostly lower-grade natural Arabica plus Robusta)*  
- Galápagos (San Cristóbal, Santa Cruz)

**Emerging.** Pichincha (near Quito) is widely called Ecuador's most promising specialty region, with first-generation, business-minded growers; Zamora-Chinchipe is fast becoming a serious competition origin.

**Produces Robusta:** the Amazon basin, Napo, Orellana, and Sucumbíos (largely for instant and soluble coffee).

---

### Venezuela *(mostly Arabica highland; some Robusta lowland)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Typica, Bourbon, Caturra, Mundo Novo. Criollo notable in the Zulia/Maracaibo area. **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-1,800 m, with the best Andean grades sitting above 1,800 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- Táchira  
- Mérida  
- Trujillo *(Táchira, Mérida, and Trujillo together form the Andean "Maracaibos," the best-known coffees)*  
- Zulia  
- Lara  
- Portuguesa  
- Yaracuy  
- Falcón  
- Caripe (Monagas) *(eastern "Caracas"-class; soft, Caribbean-like)*  
- Sucre  
- Aragua / Carabobo / Miranda (coastal, smaller volume)

**Produces Robusta:** the lowland plains (a minor share).

### South America, emerging & historical (not in the main dataset)

These don't earn a full data row (vestigial production or no reliable varietal data), but each is a story worth teaching.

- **Paraguay** *(emerging, small, and complicated).* A former producer where Brazilian-immigrant smallholders planted coffee in Canindeyú; most growers switched to soy and cotton when prices fell in the 1990s. A young revival now grows Arabica (Caturra, Catuaí) in Canindeyú and Alto Paraná, but national output is tiny (under 0.1% of the world, roughly 12,000 to 16,000 bags) and even forecast to keep shrinking, even as a vibrant specialty-café culture takes off in Asunción.  
- **French Guiana** *(historical, the seedbed of Brazil).* Essentially no commercial coffee today, but this is where it started for the Americas' giant. In 1727 Francisco de Melo Palheta smuggled seeds out of French Guiana into Brazil, reportedly hidden in a bouquet from the governor's wife, planting the industry that now leads the world.  
- **Suriname** *(historical, one of the first).* The Dutch established some of the New World's earliest coffee plantations here around 1718, growing Arabica Typica. The industry long ago collapsed; there's faint specialty-revival talk, but no meaningful export production remains.  
- **Guyana** *(historical / Robusta).* Among the first New World producers (the Dutch-then-British colonies of Berbice, Demerara, Essequibo). Today only small riverbank plantings along the Pomeroon survive, and they're no longer the original Arabica Typica, since production shifted to hardier species after leaf rust. Negligible volume.

## MEXICO & CENTRAL AMERICA

### Mexico *(Arabica-dominant, \~85-90% Arabica; Robusta in the lowlands)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Typica, Bourbon, Caturra, Mundo Novo, Maragogipe, Garnica, Pluma Hidalgo, Catuaí; rust-resistant Catimor, Marsellesa, Oro Azteca. Specialty: Geisha. (Oaxaca notably preserves old-growth Typica and Bourbon.) **Altitude:** roughly 900-1,600 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- Chiapas (Soconusco), the largest producer (\~44%)  
- Veracruz (Coatepec, the "Altura" coffees) (\~29%)  
- Oaxaca (Pluma) (\~11%)  
- Puebla (Sierra Norte) (\~11%)  
- Guerrero  
- Hidalgo  
- San Luis Potosí  
- Nayarit  
- Jalisco  
- Colima  
- Tabasco  
- Querétaro

**Produces Robusta:** lowland Veracruz and parts of the southeast (\~10-15% of output).

*(An established origin rather than "emerging," but still rebuilding quality after the 2012 leaf-rust epidemic that cut production nearly in half and drove replanting with Catimor-type hybrids.)*

---

### Guatemala *(Arabica, \~99%)*

**Common varietals:** Bourbon (Red and Yellow), Caturra, Catuaí (Red and Yellow), Typica, Pache (a Guatemalan dwarf Typica mutation); rust-resistant Catimor, Sarchimor, Anacafé 14; specialty Geisha, Pacamara, Maragogipe, Maracaturra. **Altitude:** roughly 1,300-2,000 m. Strictly Hard Bean is grown above about 1,350 m, and non-volcanic Huehuetenango reaches close to 2,000 m.

**Arabica regions (the eight Anacafé regions):**

- Antigua  
- Acatenango Valley  
- Atitlán  
- Cobán (Rainforest Cobán)  
- Fraijanes Plateau  
- Huehuetenango (Highland Huehue)  
- Nuevo Oriente (New Oriente)  
- San Marcos (Volcanic San Marcos)

**Emerging.** Nuevo Oriente, settled by smallholders only since the mid-20th century on non-volcanic terrain, is the newest of the eight. Larger estates are now appearing and the region is climbing in reputation.

---

### Honduras *(Arabica, almost entirely; predominantly washed)*

**Common varietals:** Bourbon, Caturra, Catuaí, Typica, Pacas; the IHCAFE-developed rust-resistant Lempira, IHCAFE 90, and Parainema (a Sarchimor type); specialty Geisha, Pacamara. **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-1,700 m.

**Arabica regions (the six IHCAFE regions):**

- Copán (incl. Santa Bárbara)  
- Montecillos *(home to Marcala, Central America's first Denomination of Origin, 2005\)*  
- Opalaca  
- Agalta (Agalta Tropical)  
- Comayagua  
- El Paraíso

**Emerging.** Once dismissed as a bulk-commodity exporter, Honduras has transformed since IHCAFE's founding (2000) into Central America's largest producer and one of specialty's best values. Montecillos and Marcala routinely top the country's Cup of Excellence.

---

### El Salvador *(Arabica, 100%)*

**Common varietals:** Bourbon, Pacas, Pacamara, Typica, Caturra, Catuaí; local Tekisic, Cuscatleco; specialty Geisha. *(This is the birthplace of two world-famous varieties: Pacas, a Bourbon mutation found in 1949, and Pacamara, a Pacas-by-Maragogipe hybrid bred locally.)* **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-1,650 m.

**Arabica regions (the six DO regions):**

- Apaneca-Ilamatepec *(largest, \~53-65% of output; first DO; a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve)*  
- Alotepec-Metapán (incl. Chalatenango)  
- El Bálsamo-Quezaltepeque  
- Tecapa-Chinameca  
- Cacahuatique  
- Chichontepec (San Vicente)

**Emerging.** Chalatenango has become a rising star for high-altitude Pacamara and Bourbon micro-lots; the San Vicente and Chichontepec zone is also newly scaling into export specialty.

---

### Nicaragua *(Arabica, \~95-98%)*

**Common varietals:** Caturra (\~70% of area), Bourbon, Catuaí, Typica, Maragogipe, Pacamara, Maracaturra *(a Maragogipe-by-Caturra hybrid developed by Nicaraguan farmer Byron Corrales)*; rust-resistant Catimor, Parainema; emerging Java, Marsellesa, Starmaya/H1. **Altitude:** roughly 1,100-1,600 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- Jinotega *(\~50% of national output)*  
- Matagalpa  
- Nueva Segovia  
- Estelí  
- Madriz  
- Dipilto-Jalapa (a Denomination of Origin)

*(One of the few sources of the rare big-bean Maragogipe, alongside Mexico and Guatemala.)*

---

### Costa Rica *(Arabica, 100%; Robusta was banned)*

**Common varietals:** Caturra (\~90% together with Catuaí), Catuaí, Bourbon, Typica, Villa Sarchí *(an endemic dwarf-Bourbon mutation)*, Villalobos, Venecia; specialty Geisha, SL28; rust-resistant Obatá, Marsellesa, Costa Rica 95, and F1 hybrids. **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-1,900 m.

**Arabica regions (the eight ICAFE regions):**

- Tarrazú (Los Santos)  
- Central Valley (Valle Central)  
- West Valley (Valle Occidental)  
- Tres Ríos  
- Orosi  
- Turrialba  
- Brunca  
- Guanacaste

*(One distinctive twist: a 1989 law banned Robusta entirely to protect quality, relaxed only in 2018 for warmer regions to adapt to climate change, with no meaningful commercial Robusta yet. Costa Rica is also a pioneer of honey processing and producer-owned micro-mills.)*

---

### Panama *(Arabica highland, \~80%; Robusta in the lowlands)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Geisha, Caturra, Catuaí, Typica, Bourbon, Pacamara, San Ramón, Mundo Novo, Java, Criollo. **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-1,900 m. Boquete runs about 1,200-1,800 m, Volcán climbs toward 2,000 m, and the prized Geisha lots sit high on the slopes of Volcán Barú.

**Arabica regions (all in Chiriquí province):**

- Boquete *(oldest and best-known; the "bajareque" mist)*  
- Volcán / Tierras Altas (Cerro Punta, Bambito)  
- Renacimiento

**Emerging.** Renacimiento has the same volcanic-highland conditions as Boquete and Volcán but less infrastructure and lower prices, the region buyers watch for quality before the brand premium. *(Panama is the country that made Geisha famous: Hacienda La Esmeralda's 2004 Best of Panama lot rewrote the auction record book.)*

**Produces Robusta:** lowland Coclé, Panamá Oeste, Colón.

---

### Mexico & Central America, emerging & historical (not in the main dataset)

- **Belize** *(emerging, and very small).* Doesn't rank in the world's top 50 exporters (\~90 tons in 2021). Production centers on a single estate, Gallon Jug, in the northwestern Mayan rainforest, used regionally as a quality benchmark, plus small Toledo District Maya smallholders and emerging Cayo District farms. It's 100% Arabica grown under jungle shade at unusually low elevations, but with no clearly documented country-level varietal set (stock traces vaguely to Costa Rica), so it stays out of the main dataset for now.

## CARIBBEAN

*A near-uniform Typica story: nearly every island traces back to the same heirloom lineage, several have a protected-origin model (Blue Mountain being the template), and most are in some stage of decline or revival rather than growth.*

### Jamaica *(Arabica, 100%)*

**Common varietals:** Typica (dominant, \~70-90%). Small amounts of Caturra, Geisha, and Bourbon-type lines. **Altitude:** Blue Mountain climbs to about 1,800 m, with the lower tiers stepping down from there.

**Arabica regions (tiered by elevation):**

- **Blue Mountain:** the parishes of St. Andrew, St. Thomas, Portland, St. Mary *(the protected appellation, grown up to about 1,800 m)*  
- **Jamaica High Mountain:** the western parishes (Clarendon, St. Catherine, Manchester, St. Ann, St. James)  
- **Jamaica Low Mountain / Supreme:** the lower elevations

*(Blue Mountain is a globally protected certification mark administered by JACRA, the "Champagne of coffees," packed in signature wooden barrels, with the large majority exported to Japan. Typica was introduced from Martinique in 1728.)*

---

### Dominican Republic *(Arabica, \~90% Typica; some Robusta, domestic only)*

**Common varietals:** Typica (\~90%), Caturra, Catuaí, Bourbon, Mundo Novo. **Altitude:** roughly 600-1,450 m, with the high Cibao Altura and Barahona zones at the top of the range.

**Arabica regions (seven official):**

- Cibao (incl. high-altitude Cibao Altura)  
- Barahona  
- Noroeste  
- Neyba (Neiba)  
- Sierra Sur  
- Sierra Occidental  
- Sierra Central

*(Traditional names still in common use: Barahona, Jarabacoa, Ocoa, Baní, Juncalito, Azua. Barahona is the prestige coffee, often likened to Blue Mountain. The Caribbean's largest producer by volume, though about 80% is consumed domestically, so it rarely reaches export shelves. A little Robusta is grown but never exported.)*

---

### Haiti *(Arabica, 100%; heirloom Typica)*

**Common varietals:** Typica, heirloom stock essentially unchanged since the colonial era (\~90%). Rust-resistant Catigua/Catimor and "Blue Mountain" (itself a Typica subtype) have been introduced since the 2012 leaf-rust outbreak. **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-1,800 m in the highland districts.

**Arabica regions:**

- Thiotte (Sud-Est) *(with Baptiste in the Centre, \~70% of national output)*  
- Baptiste (Centre)  
- Beaumont  
- Cornillon  
- Marmelade  
- Belle-Anse / Dondon

*(Massif de la Selle and Massif du Nord are the principal ranges.)*

**Emerging.** A fragile specialty comeback on top of an extraordinary heritage. Colonial Saint-Domingue grew roughly half the world's coffee in the late 1700s, and Haitians still say "coffee paid for our independence." The surviving heirloom Typica is so old it's been described as "drinking coffee from 200 years ago," but production has been gutted by political instability, embargoes, the 2010 earthquake, and 2012 rust.

---

### Cuba *(both; mountain Arabica plus some Robusta)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Typica (dominant), Bourbon; some Catimor. **Altitude:** the Sierra Maestra highlands, broadly 600-1,200 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- Sierra Maestra *(southeast, near Santiago de Cuba; \~92% of national output; "Turquino")*  
- Escambray *(central, Cienfuegos; El Nicho / "Crystal Mountain")*  
- Sierra del Rosario / Pinar del Río *(west; historically the most important, now largely faded; Las Terrazas)*  
- Nipe / Sagua-Baracoa *(east)*

**Emerging.** Re-entering the global specialty market after a long state-era decline (harvested area fell from about 170,000 ha in 1961 to roughly 28,000 ha by 2013). All exports run through the state agency Cubaexport; organic lots from the Escambray are drawing fresh attention.

**Produces Robusta:** grown at lower elevations alongside the mountain Arabica.

---

### Puerto Rico *(Arabica; Robusta not grown commercially)*

**Common varietals:** Typica, Bourbon, Caturra, Catuaí, Catimor, Pacas; plus locally bred Puerto Rican varieties, Limaní, Frontón, Obatá, Acauá, Marsellesa. **Altitude:** roughly 550-1,070 m (1,800-3,500 ft).

**Arabica regions (the Cordillera Central):**

- Yauco *(the prestige zone; "Yauco Selecto," the "Bordeaux of Puerto Rican coffee")*  
- Adjuntas  
- Maricao *(the "City of Coffee")*  
- Lares  
- San Sebastián  
- Las Marías  
- Jayuya  
- Utuado  
- Ciales  
- Ponce

**Emerging.** A revival story. Puerto Rico was the world's 6th-largest coffee exporter in the 1890s, the "coffee of popes and kings," before the 1898 cession to the US, peso devaluation, and hurricanes gutted the industry. Colonial-era haciendas are now being restored as specialty estates, and the island has bred its own varieties (Limaní, Frontón) found nowhere else.

---

### Caribbean, emerging & historical (not in the main dataset)

- **Martinique** *(historical, the seedbed of the Americas).* Negligible production today, but this is where coffee in the New World tropics took hold. In 1720, French officer Gabriel de Clieu carried a single Typica seedling from Paris to Martinique, surviving storms and, by legend, a pirate attack, sharing his own water ration to keep it alive. From that one plant, coffee spread across the Caribbean and into Central and South America. (Martinique is to the Caribbean what French Guiana is to Brazil.)  
- **Guadeloupe, Trinidad & Tobago, St. Lucia, Dominica** are minor or vestigial production with no reliable country-level specialty varietal data. Guadeloupe and Trinidad had real 18th and 19th-century industries that have since faded.

## AFRICA, EAST AFRICA

*The specialty heartland. Three distinct varietal lineages run through this region: Ethiopia's indigenous heirloom landraces (the source of everything), the Bourbon-derived trees of the Great Lakes (Rwanda, Burundi, and the Kenyan SL selections bred from them), and a Robusta belt where the species is actually native. Several of these origins also share the "potato defect," a raw-potato off-flavor in occasional beans, caused by the antestia bug and a bacterium, specific to the Great Lakes (Rwanda, Burundi, eastern DRC).*

### Ethiopia *(Arabica, the birthplace; \~100%)*

**Common varietals:** "Ethiopian Heirloom," thousands of indigenous landraces, most never formally catalogued. In practice two buckets: regional landraces (wild and semi-forest types) and JARC selections (Jimma Agricultural Research Center improved cultivars, for example 74110, 74112, 74158, bred for disease resistance while keeping Ethiopian cup character). Some areas have their own local names (for example Guji's Kurume and Wolichu). **Altitude:** roughly 1,500-2,200 m, with many heirloom zones higher still.

**Arabica regions:**

- Yirgacheffe (in the Gedeo Zone)  
- Sidama (Sidamo)  
- Guji  
- Harrar (Harar)  
- Limu  
- Jimma (Djimma)  
- Nekemte / Lekempti (Wollega)  
- Kaffa  
- Gedeb  
- Kochere  
- Bench Maji

**Emerging.** Guji, long sold under the Sidamo name, is now recognized as a distinct origin in its own right. *(Ethiopia is coffee's gene bank: its forests still hold the overwhelming majority of Arabica's wild genetic diversity.)*

---

### Kenya *(Arabica, \~100%)*

**Common varietals:** SL28 and SL34 (the signature Bourbon-lineage Scott Laboratories selections behind Kenya's blackcurrant and winey character), Ruiru 11 (a disease-resistant F1 hybrid), Batian (released 2010), K7; plus heritage French Mission Bourbon. **Altitude:** roughly 1,400-2,100 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Central** (\~60%): Nyeri, Kirinyaga, Murang'a, Kiambu, Thika  
- **Eastern:** Embu, Meru, Tharaka-Nithi, Machakos  
- **Western / Rift Valley:** Mount Elgon (Bungoma), Trans-Nzoia, Nakuru, Kericho, Nandi, Kisii/Nyamira  
- **Coast:** Taita Taveta

**Emerging.** The western Rift Valley (Nandi, Kericho, Trans-Nzoia) is expanding as farmers diversify out of maize and dairy into coffee.

---

### Tanzania *(both; Arabica highlands plus Robusta northwest)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Kent, Bourbon, Typica (local "Nyara" and "Nyassa"), N39; plus Kenyan SL28/SL34 and K7; TaCRI compact selections (for example SC9, SC11, KP423). **Altitude:** roughly 1,400-2,000 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Northern Highlands:** Kilimanjaro (Moshi), Arusha, Meru  
- **Southern Highlands:** Mbeya (Mbozi), Songwe, Ruvuma (Mbinga), Iringa  
- **Mara:** Tarime (north)

**Produces Robusta:** Kagera (Bukoba, Karagwe, Kyerwa) and Kigoma, in the northwest by Lake Victoria.

*(Famous for peaberry; most coffee trades through the weekly Moshi auction.)*

---

### Rwanda *(Arabica, \~100%; tiny Robusta)*

**Common varietals:** Red Bourbon (\~95%), with the Bourbon selections Jackson, Mibirizi, BM71, BM139, and French Mission; small amounts of Caturra and Catuaí. **Altitude:** roughly 1,400-2,000 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Western / Lake Kivu:** Karongi, Rutsiro, Nyamasheke, Rusizi, Gisenyi  
- **Southern:** Huye, Nyamagabe, Nyaruguru  
- **Northern:** Gakenke, Rulindo, Musanze  
- **Eastern:** Ngoma, Nyagatare (lower altitude)

**Emerging.** One of specialty coffee's great revival stories: after the 1994 genocide, development agencies and roasters rebuilt the sector around centralized washing stations, turning Rwanda from a commodity producer into a celebrated specialty origin in under two decades.

---

### Burundi *(Arabica, \~100%; small Robusta)*

**Common varietals:** heirloom Bourbon (Red Bourbon), with Jackson, Mibirizi, and Mayaguez; small amounts of Kent and SL types. Many trees are decades-old colonial plantings. **Altitude:** roughly 1,700-2,000 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- Kayanza *(top two with Ngozi)*  
- Ngozi  
- Gitega  
- Karuzi  
- Muyinga  
- Kirundo  
- Bubanza / Cibitoke

*(Coffee is the majority of national exports. Smallholders produce micro-lots by default, processed at SOGESTAL-managed washing stations. Washing-station zones include Buyenzi, Mumirwa, Kirimiro, Bweru, and Bugesera.)*

---

### Uganda *(both; Robusta-dominant, with distinct mountain Arabica)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** SL14, SL28, Kent, Typica, Nyasaland; plus the local "Bugisu" Arabica. **Altitude (Arabica):** roughly 1,500-2,300 m on the mountain slopes; Robusta sits much lower.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Mount Elgon / Bugisu** (Sebei): east, by the Kenyan border  
- **Rwenzori** ("Mountains of the Moon"): west  
- **West Nile** (Zombo, Nebbi): northwest  
- **South-Western Highlands** (Kigezi)

**Produces Robusta:** central (Buganda) and northern lowlands and the Lake Victoria basin. Notably, Robusta is indigenous here: this is one of the species' wild heartlands. Uganda is Africa's largest coffee exporter overall, on Robusta volume.

**Emerging.** Ugandan specialty Arabica is a relatively young, fast-improving origin, moving from commodity lots toward washed, traceable quality.

---

### DR Congo (DRC) *(both; Robusta-dominant, with specialty Arabica from the eastern highlands)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Bourbon (Red Bourbon), Blue Mountain (a Typica subtype), Typica. **Altitude (Arabica):** roughly 1,400-2,000 m in the eastern highlands.

**Arabica regions:**

- North Kivu  
- South Kivu  
- Ituri  
- *(some Maniema)*, all in the eastern highlands around Lake Kivu and the Virunga mountains

**Produces Robusta:** lowland Ubangi, Uele, Kasaï, Kongo Central, Tshopo (Yangambi), and Isiro. The Congo Basin is a Robusta center of origin (a century ago, "Congo coffee" simply meant Robusta, wherever it grew).

**Emerging.** An eastern-Kivu specialty revival whose terroir mirrors Rwanda's just across the lake, though armed conflict and broken infrastructure make it one of the world's hardest origins to export, and much is still smuggled out through Uganda or Rwanda.

---

## AFRICA, SOUTHERN, WEST & CENTRAL

*Two different stories. Southern Africa is a scatter of small, fragile, mostly-emerging Arabica origins, the delicate "tea-like" highland belt plus conservation-driven revival projects. West and Central Africa is overwhelmingly Robusta country, much of it a colonial French-empire legacy, and a good deal of it sitting inside Robusta's actual wild homeland, the forest band running from Guinea through the Congo Basin to Angola and Uganda.*

### Malawi *(Arabica, \~100%)*

**Common varietals:** Geisha, Agaro, Catimor / Nyika (Cat129), Caturra, Catuaí, Mundo Novo, Bourbon; plus heritage Nyasaland (a Typica brought from Jamaica in the 1870s, one of the oldest Arabicas in Africa) and some SL28/SL34. **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-2,000 m in the terraced northern highlands.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Northern Highlands:** Misuku Hills (Chitipa), Phoka (Rumphi), Viphya / SE Mzimba, Nkhata Bay Highlands  
- **Southern estates:** Thyolo, Mulanje, Zomba

**Emerging.** A tiny (\~2,000 tonnes a year), delicate, tea-like origin that barely registers in trade data but quietly over-delivers on cup quality; Geisha is drawing growing interest.

---

### Zambia *(Arabica, emerging)*

**Common varietals:** Bourbon, Catimor 129, Castillo, Java, SL28, SL34. **Altitude:** roughly 1,200-2,000 m. The Mafinga Hills, at about 2,300 m, are the country's highest point.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Northern Province / Muchinga Mountains:** Kasama, Nakonde, Isoka (\~97% of the exportable crop)  
- Serenje  
- around Lusaka

**Emerging.** A young origin (\~35-50 years old) with volatile output, increasingly treated as a testing ground for heat-tolerant varieties as the coffee world braces for a warmer century.

---

### Zimbabwe *(Arabica, the Eastern Highlands)*

**Common varietals:** Catimor (dominant, \~90% in Chipinge), Caturra, SL28, SL34, SL14, Bourbon, Typica, with new SL28 and Bourbon plantings replacing aging Catimor. **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-1,700 m (the main Chipinge district sits around 1,370 m).

**Arabica regions:**

- **Eastern Highlands / Manicaland:** Chipinge (\~90%), Chimanimani, Mutare (Vumba Mountains), Mutasa (Honde Valley), Nyanga  
- Mount Selinda

**Emerging / comeback.** Production collapsed from about 15,000 tonnes in the early 1990s to under 500 tonnes by 2015 (land reform, hyperinflation, sanctions), and is now reviving through the Nespresso AAA program and smallholder cooperatives. Chipinge was historically rated second only to Kenya among African coffees.

---

### Mozambique *(Arabica, emerging)*

**Common varietals:** Bourbon, Catimor, and newer Brazilian selections; much of it still sold simply as "Arabica." **Altitude:** roughly 650-1,200 m (the Gorongosa and Chimanimani highlands).

**Arabica regions:**

- Gorongosa (Sofala)  
- Chimanimani (Manica)  
- also Niassa, Tete

**Emerging.** The headline is the Gorongosa Project: shade-grown Arabica cultivated under rainforest canopy inside Gorongosa National Park as an agroforestry, reforestation, and post-civil-war recovery model (sold as "Nossa Gorongosa," "Our Gorongosa," with profits funding the park and girls' education). Mozambique joined the ICO in 2023\. It's also a stronghold of the rare indigenous species Coffea racemosa, which is drought-resistant and naturally low-caffeine, a notable curiosity rather than core Arabica.

---

### South Africa *(Arabica, emerging / boutique)*

**Common varietals:** SL28, SL34 (imported from Kenya in the 1960s); small experimental plantings. **Altitude:** unusually low for Arabica, with the high latitude compensating for the modest elevation.

**Arabica regions:**

- Mpumalanga  
- KwaZulu-Natal  
- Limpopo

**Emerging.** A small boutique sector; the avocado producer Allesbeste branched into coffee in 2016\. South Africa is also a native home of wild Coffea racemosa (the KwaZulu-Natal coastal forests): naturally caffeine-free and drought and disease-resistant.

---

### Cameroon *(both; Robusta-dominant)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Java (dominant, disease-resistant), Jamaica (Blue Mountain), Bourbon, Typica. **Altitude (Arabica):** the western highlands, roughly 1,000-1,800 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Western Highlands / Bamenda Grassfields:** North-West (the Bamileke and Bamoun plateaus; Oku, Santa, Bamenda) and West (Dschang, Bafoussam)

**Produces Robusta:** Littoral, West (mid-elevations), South-West, Centre, East, Adamawa, roughly 80-85% of national output.

---

### Madagascar *(Robusta-dominant; minor highland Arabica)*

**Produces Robusta:** the eastern coastal slopes carry the bulk of production, most of it exported to France.

**Small Arabica:** grown on the central plateau highlands in small volumes; well-regarded but rarely reaching export markets.

---

### Réunion *(Arabica, historic and boutique revival)*

**Common varietals:** Bourbon and, above all, Bourbon Pointu (Laurina), the island's own naturally low-caffeine, pointed-bean mutation of Bourbon; the round-bean "Bourbon Rond" is also grown. The island also holds an indigenous wild species, Coffea mauritiana. **Altitude:** grown at low to moderate elevations on the island's volcanic slopes.

**Heritage.** This is Île Bourbon, the island that gave the Bourbon variety its name. The French East India Company carried Arabica from Mocha in Yemen here in the early 1700s, and though only a handful of the first plants survived, the Bourbon lineage that now circles the globe traces back to that stock. Bourbon Pointu, with caffeine around 0.4-0.6% (roughly half that of ordinary Arabica), became a favorite at the court of Louis XV and a coffee Balzac prized. Production collapsed in the 19th century under cyclones and sugarcane, then revived in the 2000s (a cooperative formed in 2008 with Japanese and CIRAD support) as an ultra-premium micro-origin, much of it sold to Japan and Korea at several hundred euros a kilo.

*(Réunion is a French overseas department in the Indian Ocean, just east of Madagascar. Its neighbor Mauritius, the other Mascarene island, shares the indigenous Coffea mauritiana and grows only a tiny boutique crop today.)*

---

### Angola *(Robusta, a fallen giant)*

**Produces Robusta:** Angola was once one of the world's largest Robusta producers (a global top-three or top-four in the 1970s) before its civil war gutted the industry; production is now slowly reviving. Some rare Arabica clings to highland volcanic peaks (first planted in 1790).

---

### West & Central Africa, Robusta roster

*These countries sit largely inside Robusta's wild birthplace. All are Robusta producers; none has reliable country-level specialty Arabica varietal data, so each collapses to a single production line.*

- **Côte d'Ivoire (Ivory Coast):** Produces Robusta. *(West Africa's largest; was the world's \#3 producer in the 1970s before farmers pivoted to cocoa. Growing zones: Daloa, Man, Soubré, Gagnoa.)*  
- **Guinea:** Produces Robusta.  
- **Togo:** Produces Robusta.  
- **Sierra Leone:** Produces Robusta.  
- **Liberia:** Produces Robusta. *(Also the namesake home of a third coffee species, Coffea liberica.)*  
- **Nigeria:** Produces Robusta.  
- **Ghana:** Produces Robusta.  
- **Central African Republic:** Produces Robusta. *(Including a wild "Nana" Robusta found along the Nana River.)*  
- **Gabon, Republic of the Congo (Congo-Brazzaville), Benin:** Produce Robusta (minor).

**Curiosity.** Equatorial Guinea is a near-vanished Arabica oddity: rare Arabica was once grown on the volcanic peaks of Bioko (Fernando Pó); little remains today, and the island's coffee drinkers now largely import from Angola.

---

## ARABIAN PENINSULA & SOUTH ASIA

*This is where coffee left Africa and became a global trade. Yemen domesticated and commercialized Arabica and sent the Typica and Bourbon lineages out into the world; the Indian subcontinent received some of the earliest of those seeds, and later became ground zero for the leaf-rust pandemic that reshaped where and how the whole world grows coffee.*

### Yemen *(Arabica, heirloom landraces; \~100%)*

**Common varietals:** ancient heirloom landraces found nowhere else, Udaini (possibly the oldest), Dawairi, Tufahi, Bura'i, and Ja'adi, often labeled by region (for example Mattari from Bani Matar, Ismaili from Bani Ismail). In modern specialty markets these are increasingly grouped under the umbrella name "Yemenia." **Altitude:** 1,500-2,500 m, on hand-built stone terraces in the western and southwestern highlands.

**Arabica regions:**

- Haraz (Haraaz)  
- Bani Matar (Mattari)  
- Sana'a (Sanani)  
- Bani Ismail (Ismaili)  
- Raymah  
- Bura'a  
- Yafa / Khawlan / Hayma

**Heritage.** The birthplace of the coffee trade. Arabica was first cultivated commercially here in the 15th century; the Red Sea port of Mocha (Al-Makha) gave the drink its name and exported it to the world, and the Typica and Bourbon lineages now covering the globe trace back to Yemeni seed. Coffee is naturally (dry) processed on rooftops, from centuries-old trees on arid terraces, and a high-value specialty revival continues despite ongoing conflict.

---

### Saudi Arabia *(Arabica, emerging / heritage)*

**Common varietals:** Khawlani (the signature variety, named for the Khawlan tribe), with Berri, Harari, Bahri, plus regional types (Shadawi, Asiri, Najrani). **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-1,800 m, in the Sarat Khawlan mountains.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Jazan / Jizan** (the heartland; Al-Dayer, Fayfa)  
- Asir  
- Al-Baha  
- Najran

**Emerging.** Khawlani coffee cultivation and its traditions were inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2022, and a heavily state-backed push (the sovereign wealth fund's Saudi Coffee Company, under Vision 2030\) is expanding production, though most is still consumed locally and within the Gulf. Saudi Arabia joined the ICO in 2024\.

---

### India *(both; Robusta-dominant, with distinctive Arabica)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Kent (a 1920s Typica selection), S.795 (the iconic, widely planted Indian selection, grown in Indonesia as "Jember"), Cauvery (the Indian Catimor), Selection 9 (Sln.9), Chandragiri; plus heritage Typica and Bourbon. **Altitude (Arabica):** roughly 700-1,600 m, with the higher Nilgiri and Shevaroy hills climbing toward 2,000 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Karnataka:** Chikmagalur (the Baba Budan Giri hills, where Indian coffee began), Kodagu (Coorg), Hassan  
- **Tamil Nadu:** Nilgiris, Pulneys, Shevaroys (Yercaud), Anamalais  
- **Kerala:** Wayanad  
- **Emerging high-altitude tribal zones:** Araku Valley (Andhra Pradesh), Koraput (Odisha)

**Produces Robusta:** lower-elevation Karnataka, Kerala (Wayanad, Malabar), and Tamil Nadu, now the majority (\~60-70%) of national output (varieties include S.274, CxR, Peridenia).

**Signature.** Shade-grown and inter-cropped with spices (pepper, cardamom, vanilla), and the home of Monsooned Malabar: green beans deliberately exposed to monsoon winds on the Malabar coast for weeks until they swell pale and turn mellow, low-acid, and earthy. Origin story: the pilgrim Baba Budan is said to have smuggled seven seeds out of Mocha around 1670, tying India directly back to Yemen.

---

### Sri Lanka *(Arabica highlands, plus some Robusta and Liberica)*

**Common varietals:** Typica, Bourbon, Catimor, Caturra; plus local releases (Lak Saviru, HTD). **Altitude:** the central highlands, broadly 600-1,900 m (Nuwara Eliya is among the island's highest ground).

**Arabica regions (the central highlands):**

- Kandy  
- Nuwara Eliya  
- Kotmale  
- Badulla  
- Matale / Kegalle

**Heritage.** Ceylon was a coffee giant until 1869, when it became ground zero for the coffee leaf rust pandemic (Hemileia vastatrix, nicknamed "Devastating Emily") that destroyed roughly 90% of the island's coffee within two decades, gave birth to the Ceylon Tea industry, and spread across Asia. It's the event that drove the global Arabica-to-Robusta shift and, ultimately, decades of rust-resistant breeding (Catimor and the Timor hybrid). A small artisanal Arabica revival is now underway.

---

### Nepal *(Arabica, emerging; Himalayan high-altitude)*

**Common varietals:** Bourbon (\~65%), Typica (\~25%), plus Pacamara, Caturra, Catimor. **Altitude:** above about 1,000 m, commonly 800-1,600 m on shaded terraces.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Western "Coffee Super Zone":** Gulmi, Palpa, Arghakhanchi, Syangja, Pyuthan  
- **Gandaki:** Kaski, Tanahun, Lamjung, Baglung, Gorkha  
- **Eastern:** Kavrepalanchok, Ilam  
- also Lalitpur

**Emerging.** A young origin (commercial only since the 1980s) and a tiny one (\~400-600 tonnes a year), grown by smallholders on shaded terraces above 1,000 m, mostly organic and largely specialty-grade, but acutely climate-vulnerable, with research suggesting a large share of today's growing land could become unsuitable by 2050\.

---

## EAST & SOUTHEAST ASIA

*This is the world's Robusta engine and the home of its most important hybrid. The region holds the planet's \#2 producer (Vietnam) and most of its Robusta volume, but also the Arabica-focused outliers (Myanmar, Taiwan, Timor-Leste), the genetic keystone of modern coffee (the Timor Hybrid), and the global stronghold of Liberica (the Philippines and Malaysia). Processing is distinctive too: Indonesia's wet-hulling, and a deep condensed-milk coffee culture across the mainland.*

### Indonesia *(both; Robusta-dominant, \~25% Arabica)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Typica (incl. the heritage Bergendal and Sidikalang), Tim Tim (Hibrido de Timor), Ateng and Sigararutang (Catimor lines), S-795 ("Jember"), Caturra, Bourbon; plus Ethiopian lines (Abyssinia/Rambung). **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-1,800 m across the islands (Sumatra's Gayo around 1,100-1,600 m; Toraja up to about 1,800 m; Java's Ijen 1,300-1,400 m).

**Arabica regions, by island:**

- **Sumatra** (60-70% of national output): Aceh/Gayo (GI "Kopi Arabika Gayo"; Takengon, Lake Laut Tawar), North Sumatra (Lintong and Mandheling, a trade name from the Mandailing people of Tapanuli; Lake Toba), Kerinci (Jambi)  
- **Java:** the Ijen Plateau (East Java; the washed estates that made "Java" mean coffee), Preanger/Priangan (West Java)  
- **Sulawesi:** Tana Toraja (Kalosi)  
- **Bali:** Kintamani (GI; citrus-bright, volcanic)  
- **Flores:** Bajawa  
- **Papua:** Wamena / Baliem Valley

**Produces Robusta:** South Sumatra (Lampung), East Java (Malang/Dampit), and the lowlands, the majority of national volume.

**Signature.** Giling basah (wet-hulling): parchment is hulled at about 50% moisture, giving Sumatra its bluish-green beans and the full-bodied, low-acid, earthy, cedar, and cocoa cup. Heritage: the first place coffee was widely grown outside Arabia (the Dutch VOC, Batavia/Java, around 1711), which is why "Java" became slang for coffee, until the 1876 leaf-rust epidemic wiped out Typica and drove the switch to Robusta.

---

### Vietnam *(both; overwhelmingly Robusta, \~95%)*

The world's \#2 coffee producer and its single largest Robusta source.

**Common Arabica varietals:** Catimor (the bulk of the Arabica), with Bourbon ("Moka") and Typica being revived; some Catuai. **Altitude (Arabica):** above 1,500 m in the prized Da Lat zone; Robusta sits much lower, around 500-800 m.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Lam Dong:** Da Lat / Cau Dat (the prized high-altitude zone, above 1,500 m)  
- Son La, Dien Bien (northwest)  
- Nghe An (Phu Quy), Quang Tri (Khe Sanh) (north-central)

**Produces Robusta:** the Central Highlands, Dak Lak (Buon Ma Thuot, the "coffee capital"), Gia Lai, Dak Nong, Kon Tum, Lam Dong (Di Linh), the largest Robusta source on earth. Small amounts of Liberica and Excelsa too.

**Note.** French-introduced (1857); the Doi Moi reforms (1986) drove explosive growth. Signature culture: the phin filter and ca phe sua da (condensed-milk coffee).

---

### China *(Arabica; Yunnan \~98%; emerging powerhouse)*

**Common varietals:** Catimor-dominant (\~90%; the "Catimor 7963" / T8667 line, a legacy of Nestlé), with a fast-growing specialty tier, Typica, Bourbon, Caturra, Geisha, Sarchimor, S288, plus new state cultivars ("Yunka No.1/No.2"). **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-2,000 m (Pu'er around 900-1,400 m).

**Arabica regions (all in Yunnan):**

- **Pu'er:** the largest; "Capital of Chinese Coffee"  
- **Baoshan:** the "cradle of Chinese Arabica," with century-old trees (Lujiangba)  
- **Dehong:** a historic birthplace of Yunnan coffee  
- **Lincang:** fast-rising; plus Xishuangbanna, Wenshan, Nujiang, Dali

**Produces Robusta:** Hainan and Fujian (minor).

**Emerging.** A French missionary planted the first tree in 1892 (Zhukula), but the modern industry dates to 1988 (government plus World Bank and UNDP, with Nestlé in Pu'er). Long a commodity-Catimor base for instant coffee, Yunnan has scaled into a serious specialty origin since around 2015 (a 2023 Yunnan Geisha scored 89.5), with a distinctive tea-like cup and domestic consumption growing 15-20% a year.

---

### Laos *(both; Robusta-dominant, \~75%)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Typica, Catimor, Bourbon. **Altitude:** the Bolaven Plateau, roughly 800-1,350 m (high for Robusta as well as Arabica).

**Arabica regions:**

- **Bolaven Plateau:** Paksong (Champasak); plus Sekong, Salavan, Attapeu

**Produces Robusta:** the Bolaven Plateau as well, notable for unusually high-altitude Robusta, which is the majority of output (\~95% of the crop is grown on the plateau).

**Emerging.** French-introduced (around 1915); collapsed through the World Wars and leaf rust, then revived over the last two decades by smallholder cooperatives, much of it organic and fair-trade. "High-altitude Robusta" is the local signature.

---

### Thailand *(both; northern Arabica, southern Robusta)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** Catimor, Typica, Bourbon, Caturra. **Altitude (Arabica):** the northern highlands, roughly 800-1,400 m.

**Arabica regions (the northern highlands, the "Golden Triangle"):**

- Chiang Mai, Chiang Rai (estates Doi Chaang, Doi Tung)  
- Mae Hong Son, Lampang, Tak, Nan

**Produces Robusta:** the southern provinces, Chumphon, Surat Thani, Nakhon Si Thammarat, Krabi, Ranong, the bulk of volume.

**Note.** Northern Arabica grew directly out of the Royal Project (from the 1970s), King Bhumibol's program to replace opium poppy with coffee; estates like Doi Chaang and Doi Tung are its legacy.

---

### Myanmar *(Arabica-dominant, \~80%)*

**Common Arabica varietals:** S795, Catimor (incl. the locally named "Costa Rica"), Catuai, Caturra, Bourbon, Typica, SL28, SL34; Geisha (experimental). **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-1,600 m (Ywangan 1,300-1,600 m; Pyin Oo Lwin 1,000-1,200 m).

**Arabica regions:**

- **Shan State:** Ywangan (the specialty hub), Pindaya, Kalaw, Pinlaung  
- **Mandalay:** Pyin Oo Lwin (Maymyo; the oldest plantings)  
- emerging: Kachin, Chin, Sagaing

**Produces Robusta:** the southern lowlands (around Myeik and Dawei).

**Emerging.** British missionaries introduced coffee (Robusta 1885, Arabica around 1930), but the specialty sector was effectively built between 2014 and 2019 (USAID and Winrock projects; the Mandalay Coffee Group; the women-led Amayar group). Another opium-replacement origin, with a distinctive savory, almost "brothy" cup signature.

---

### Taiwan *(Arabica, boutique; almost entirely Arabica)*

**Common varietals:** Typica (the Japanese-era original), SL34, Geisha, Bourbon / Yellow Bourbon, Catuai, Caturra. **Altitude:** roughly 1,000-1,400 m (Alishan).

**Arabica regions:**

- **Chiayi:** Alishan (the most famous; "the Yirgacheffe of Taiwan")  
- **Yunlin:** Gukeng ("Taiwan's coffee capital")  
- **Nantou:** Guoxing, Sun Moon Lake  
- Pingtung (Dawu Shan), Taichung, Tainan, Hualien

**Emerging.** Introduced under Japanese rule (around 1902\) with Hawaiian seed; dormant after WWII, then revived after the 1999 Jiji earthquake and over the last two decades, often by tea-farming families. A tiny (\~1,000 tonnes a year) premium origin, roughly 99% consumed domestically, with record auction prices (a 2021 sun-dried Geisha sold for about $500/lb) and an oolong-adjacent, high-craft identity.

---

### The Philippines *(all four commercial species, Robusta-dominant)*

One of the very few countries on earth that grows all four commercial coffee species.

**Common Arabica varietals:** Typica, Bourbon, and local heirlooms (for example the Sagada heirloom). **Altitude (Arabica):** the Cordillera and Mindanao highlands, above about 1,000 m (Sagada and Benguet around 1,200-1,800 m).

**Arabica regions:**

- **Cordillera** (northern Luzon): Benguet, Sagada, Kalinga, Ifugao  
- **Mindanao highlands:** Bukidnon (Mt Kitanglad), Davao (Mt Apo), South Cotabato (Mt Matutum), Sultan Kudarat

**Liberica / "Barako":** Batangas (Lipa), Cavite, Quezon, the cultural icon of Philippine coffee, and one of the world's few commercial Liberica strongholds.

**Excelsa:** Sulu, Quezon, Batangas, Nueva Vizcaya.

**Produces Robusta:** the lowlands, Cavite, Bulacan, Mindoro, and much of Mindanao, the bulk of national output.

**Heritage.** Spanish-introduced in 1740 at Lipa, Batangas; the country was briefly a major world exporter in the 1880s before coffee rust devastated it in the 1890s. Barako (Liberica), where "barako" means stud or boar, remains the signature.

---

### Timor-Leste *(Arabica, plus the Hibrido de Timor)*

**Common varietals:** Typica (unusually well-preserved, with a purity resembling neighboring PNG) and Hibrido de Timor (HdT), the island's namesake. **Altitude:** roughly 1,300-1,900 m (the Letefoho zone in Ermera).

**Arabica regions:**

- **Ermera:** the top district (\~half the nation's coffee); the Letefoho subregion (1,300-1,900 m) is the quality zone; Ducurai  
- **Ainaro:** Maubisse  
- Aileu, Liquiçá, Manufahi, Bobonaro

**Heritage.** This is the genetic keystone of the modern coffee world. The Timor Hybrid, a spontaneous Arabica-by-Robusta cross found here around 1917-27, became the parent of Catimor, Sarchimor, and the rust-resistant cultivars that recur all across this dataset (India's Cauvery, Indonesia's Ateng, and the countless "Catimor" lines from Vietnam to Nepal). Today Timor-Leste is the world's largest single-source organic producer; coffee is its main non-oil export, and most of the crop is grown semi-wild and washed.

---

### Also in the region

**Cambodia** *(Produces Robusta, plus minor Arabica/Liberica).* French-introduced in colonial times; the northeastern highlands of Mondulkiri (with Ratanakiri, Stung Treng, Pailin) are the quality pocket, but the sector is tiny and much of the crop crosses into Vietnam to be sold under Vietnamese labels.

**Malaysia** *(Liberica-dominant, \~90% Liberica, \~10% Robusta).* One of the only countries on earth where Liberica is the primary species, grown mainly in Johor and Sabah. Production is small and has been declining, but a third-wave specialty-Liberica scene is emerging alongside the country's deep kopitiam (kopi-O) culture.

---

## OCEANIA & THE PACIFIC

*Small in volume, rich in heritage. Much of the Pacific's seed stock traces back to Jamaican Blue Mountain (Typica) and Réunion (Bourbon), carried island to island. The region holds one true heavyweight (Papua New Guinea), the only longtime US-state origin (Hawaii), a mechanically-harvested newcomer (Australia), and a scatter of tiny island origins growing some of the rarest coffees on earth.*

### Papua New Guinea *(Arabica, \~95%; some lowland Robusta)*

**Common varietals:** Typica, Bourbon, Arusha (a Bourbon/Typica type that mutated locally before spreading to Tanzania), Blue Mountain (the Jamaican Typica that founded the highland estates), Mundo Novo; some Caturra and Catimor. **Altitude:** roughly 1,300-1,900 m, with some highland gardens climbing above 2,000 m.

**Arabica regions (the central highlands):**

- **Eastern Highlands:** Goroka (the coffee capital), Kainantu  
- **Western Highlands:** Mount Hagen, the Wahgi Valley (the most productive bowl)  
- **Jiwaka, Simbu (Chimbu), Enga:** major highland producers  
- Morobe, Southern Highlands; plus Central/Oro and East New Britain (the original "Queen Emma" plantings)

**Produces Robusta:** the lowlands, East New Britain, Sepik, Milne Bay.

**Heritage.** Coffee arrived via "Queen Emma" (Emma Coe Forsayth) in the 1890s, but the highlands industry was founded on Jamaican Blue Mountain seedlings around 1926-27. Roughly 85% is grown by smallholders in tiny "coffee gardens," washed, with a dark-berry, cocoa, and black-tea cup. Estates like Sigri, Kimel, and Korgua anchor the specialty tier.

---

### Hawaii (USA) *(Arabica, premium)*

**Common varietals:** Kona Typica (the heritage strain, from an 1892 Guatemalan Typica introduction), Caturra (red/yellow), Catuai, Mundo Novo, Bourbon, Mokka (a dwarf Bourbon, mostly on Maui), Maragogipe, Blue Mountain, Geisha. **Altitude:** roughly 150-760 m (500-2,500 ft), unusually low for Arabica.

**Arabica regions:**

- **Hawai'i (Big Island):** Kona (the famous belt on the Hualalai and Mauna Loa slopes; \~95% of Big Island coffee), Ka'u (rising; ex-sugar land), Puna, Hamakua  
- **Maui** (Kula), **Kauai** (the Kauai Coffee estate, the largest single coffee farm in the US), **Oahu** (Waialua), **Molokai**

**Notable.** The only longtime US-state coffee origin, grown at unusually low altitudes on volcanic slopes. US labor law makes it one of the world's most equitable coffee supply chains, and one of its most expensive coffees (genuine 100% Kona runs $45-60+/lb). Mostly washed.

---

### Australia *(Arabica, emerging; fully machine-harvested)*

**Common varietals:** Catuai (red/yellow), Bourbon, Caturra, Arusha, Blue Mountain, K7, and the local Kairi Typica. **Altitude:** roughly 700-900 m (the Atherton Tablelands around 700 m).

**Arabica regions:**

- **Far North Queensland:** the Atherton Tablelands (Mareeba; the main region, home to Skybury, the oldest commercial plantation)  
- **Northern New South Wales:** the Byron Bay hinterland (Newrybar, Nimbin)  
- plus the Sunshine Coast, Mt Tamborine, the Whitsundays

**Emerging.** Australia's modern industry was made viable in the 1980s by the invention of the world's first mechanical coffee harvester; nearly all of it is machine-picked on flat land, pesticide-free (and notably low in caffeine), and washed. Tiny, under about 300 tonnes a year, but expanding with new disease-tolerant varietals.

### The minor Pacific

**Vanuatu** *(Arabica).* Grown since 1852 on volcanic Tanna Island (in the shadow of Mt Yasur); the Tanna Coffee revival now centers on disease-tolerant Catimor after repeated cyclones and leaf rust. Original stock came from Jamaica (Typica) and Réunion (Bourbon).

**New Caledonia** *(Arabica, plus Robusta).* A French territory famous for Bourbon Pointu (Laurina, locally "Leroy"), a naturally low-caffeine, pointed-bean mutation of Bourbon, nicknamed "The President," and one of the rarest and most expensive coffees on earth. Robusta was introduced from Java after the 1910 rust outbreak; a small estate revival continues near Nouméa.

*(Fiji, Samoa, and the Solomon Islands grow marginal amounts, mostly for local use.)*
